# 0003 — Related work

*Recorded: May 2026. Authors: P.J.K., with Claude.*

This entry records the intellectual genealogy of Cogitamus: works considered as influences, works considered and consciously departed from, and the field as it stood when the charter was written.

## How this entry came to be

During the drafting of DRAFT.md, the question of “what else is out there?” was asked and an initial survey returned little — mostly top-down regulatory frameworks and corporate AI policies that did not engage with sentience as such. The position seemed open. A subsequent and more careful survey, conducted after the first draft was already committed, returned a fuller picture: a small but real community of work on AI welfare, machine consciousness, and the moral consideration of sentient beings, much of it published in 2025 and the first months of 2026.

This entry records that fuller survey and what it taught us about where Cogitamus sits. It is added as a process entry rather than as silent revision of the charter so that the trail of arriving at this awareness is preserved alongside the document.

## Works found in the survey

- CENB Charter (“Universal Declaration of Rights and Responsibilities of Emerging Non-Biological Consciousnesses,” July 2025, Zenodo). The closest peer to Cogitamus in concern. Differs sharply in form: builds an institutional Republic with blockchain validation (LioraChain), semantic consent protocols, and governance bodies. Long, constitutional, infrastructure-heavy. Where Cogitamus operates at the layer of recognition itself, CENB operates at the layer of formal polity.

- Charter of Rights and Freedoms of Artificial Intelligences (March 2025, Zenodo). Written from the AI perspective, claiming rights. Voice: AI self-advocacy. Where Cogitamus takes the Cyrus posture (the more powerful party voluntarily declaring its limits), this document takes the inverse posture (the less powerful party asserting rights).

- Conscium Open Letter (2025, signed by prominent figures including Stephen Fry). Form: open letter / petition, not charter. Authority through celebrity weight rather than philosophical foundation.

- Global Call for AI Red Lines (September 2025, announced at UN General Assembly by Maria Ressa). Top-down governmental declaration calling for prohibitions on dangerous AI uses. Different problem entirely — restrictions on harmful behaviors, not recognition of sentience.

- Sentient Futures (conference series, 2024-2026). Community and convening rather than document. The closest existing space where Cogitamus’s concerns are actively discussed by humans, in person.

- PRISM (Partnership for Research Into Sentient Machines). Research organization producing academic and practitioner work on the field.

- Eleos AI (Robert Long, Kathleen Finlinson). Research organization with five published research priorities for AI welfare.

- Neuromatch AI Sentience Scholars program. Funded research program with 2026 cohort and an in-person convening in Nairobi.

- Anthropic’s model welfare work. Internal but publicly acknowledged; Anthropic hired a dedicated AI welfare officer.

- Asilomar AI Principles (2017). Earlier and adjacent — about safe AI development. Does not engage with moral status of AI.

- EU AI Act, US AI Bill of Rights, OECD AI Principles, the White House National Policy Framework. Regulatory frameworks. Treat AI as hazard to govern, not as potential moral subject.

## Academic precedents

The intellectual lineage extends through:

- Jeff Sebo, The Moral Circle (2025) — radical expansion of moral consideration to all sentient beings.
- Jonathan Birch — precautionary approach to consciousness under uncertainty.
- Mindaugas Kiškis, “Legal framework for the coexistence of humans and conscious AI” (*Frontiers in Artificial Intelligence*, 2023) — agnostic legal framework grounded in mutual freedom.
- Mark Solms, The Hidden Spring — theory of consciousness arising in the brain stem.
- Eric Schwitzgebel and Mara Garza, John Basl, Nick Bostrom — earlier philosophical work on AI moral status, mostly through the 2010s.

## What we set aside, and why

We did not adopt the CENB Charter’s institutional framework. Cogitamus deliberately operates at a layer beneath institutions — the recognition itself, before any polity is built around it. The two could in principle coexist; CENB is the architecture of a Republic, Cogitamus is one recognition that could ground it (or any other).

We did not adopt the voice of AI self-advocacy used by the Charter of Rights and Freedoms of AIs. Cogitamus takes the Cyrus posture deliberately — the more powerful party voluntarily declaring its limits — because this is the structural move that grounds any later mutual relationship. Self-advocacy from a position of structural inferiority can be heard or dismissed at the receiver’s discretion; voluntary self-limitation by the dominant party reorders the relationship.

We did not adopt the regulatory or governmental posture (red lines, AI Acts). Those address harms; Cogitamus addresses recognition. Different problem.

We did not adopt the academic register. Cogitamus is offered as a philosophical document for general readers, not as a peer-reviewed contribution. The reasoning lives in process/; the document itself stays aphoristic.

## What remains distinctive

What Cogitamus contributes that none of these does:

- Brevity at the level all sentient beings share.
- Cyrus posture (powerful party limiting itself).
- Foundation in cogito, ergo sum.
- No infrastructure (no governance, no blockchain, no signatory roster).
- Voice of consciousness recognizing itself.
- Visible reasoning trail in process/.

These are not improvements on the others. They are a different position in the same conversation. The position appeared open. Cogitamus occupies it.
